[PUNCTUATION_AND_WRITING]
# Step 2 — What punctuation marks mean; handwriting styles; visual/practical
#           learning theory
################################################################################

PUNCTUATION_PERIOD: A period (.) ends a declarative sentence.
    Example: "The cat sat on the mat."

PUNCTUATION_COMMA: A comma (,) indicates a pause, separates list items, or
    joins clauses.  Example: "I bought apples, oranges, and bananas."

PUNCTUATION_QUESTION_MARK: A question mark (?) ends a direct question.
    Example: "What is the capital of France?"

PUNCTUATION_EXCLAMATION: An exclamation mark (!) expresses strong emotion
    or emphasis.  Example: "That is incredible!"

PUNCTUATION_COLON: A colon (:) introduces a list, explanation, or quotation.
    Example: "There are three primary colours: red, blue, and yellow."

PUNCTUATION_SEMICOLON: A semicolon (;) separates two related independent
    clauses.  Example: "The sun rose; birds began to sing."

PUNCTUATION_APOSTROPHE: An apostrophe (') indicates possession or a
    contraction.  Examples: "Alice's book" / "don't" (do not).

PUNCTUATION_HYPHEN: A hyphen (-) joins compound words or word parts.
    Example: "well-known author."

PUNCTUATION_DASH: An em dash (—) creates a strong break or parenthetical.
    Example: "He finally arrived — three hours late."

PUNCTUATION_ELLIPSIS: An ellipsis (...) indicates omitted text or a
    trailing thought.  Example: "And then..."

PUNCTUATION_QUOTATION: Quotation marks ("") enclose direct speech or
    titles.  Example: She said, "Hello."

PUNCTUATION_PARENTHESES: Parentheses () enclose supplementary information.
    Example: "The element gold (Au) is highly conductive."

PUNCTUATION_BRACKETS: Square brackets [] add editorial clarification inside
    a quotation.  Example: "He [the president] confirmed the decision."

LETTER_UPPERCASE: Uppercase letters (A–Z) begin sentences, mark proper
    nouns, and signal importance.  ALL CAPS often indicates shouting or
    high emphasis in digital communication.

LETTER_LOWERCASE: Lowercase letters (a–z) are used in normal body text.
    They are easier to read at length than all-caps.

HANDWRITING_JOINED: Joined (cursive) writing connects letters within a
    word in a flowing, continuous stroke.  It improves writing speed and
    is traditionally taught in schools.

HANDWRITING_PRINT: Print (block) writing draws each letter separately.
    It is highly legible and used in formal documents and labels.

HANDWRITING_BUBBLE: Bubble writing enlarges and rounds each letter so it
    looks like a balloon or bubble.  It is used in art, titles, and signs.

HANDWRITING_CALLIGRAPHY: Calligraphy is decorative writing using broad-
    nibbed pens or brushes.  Thick and thin strokes create elegant patterns.

HANDWRITING_ITALIC: Italic writing slants letters to the right; it is used
    for emphasis in typed text and as a style in calligraphy.

LEARNING_THEORY_VISUAL: Visual learners understand best through images,
    diagrams, charts, and written words.  Providing a file path or image
    gives a visual learner concrete reference material.

LEARNING_THEORY_PRACTICAL: Practical (kinaesthetic) learners understand
    best by doing — typing commands, running code, building things.

LEARNING_THEORY_AUDITORY: Auditory learners understand best by listening —
    verbal instructions, discussion, or reading aloud.

IMAGE_INPUT_NOTE: To provide an image, type the full file path, e.g.:
    /home/user/diagram.png
    Melvin will note the path and describe what the image represents based
    on its name and context.  (Full vision capability requires a model
    that supports image tokens, such as llava.)

[MATHEMATICS_AND_NUMBERS]
# Step 6 — History of numbers, mathematics, planets, astronomy
################################################################################

NUMBERS_ANCIENT_EGYPT: Ancient Egyptians (c. 3000 BCE) used a base-10
    hieroglyphic system.  Separate symbols represented 1, 10, 100, 1 000,
    10 000, 100 000, and 1 000 000.  The Rhind Mathematical Papyrus
    (c. 1650 BCE) shows arithmetic and geometry used for construction.

NUMBERS_BABYLONIAN: Babylonians (c. 2000 BCE) used a base-60 (sexagesimal)
    system — the origin of our 60-second minutes, 60-minute hours, and
    360-degree circles.  They understood the concept of zero as a
    placeholder before it was formalised.

NUMBERS_GREEK: Ancient Greeks (c. 600–300 BCE) used letters for numbers
    (alpha=1, beta=2, etc.) and made enormous contributions to geometry
    and logic.  Pythagoras proved the right-angle triangle theorem.
    Euclid wrote "Elements," the foundation of geometry.
    Archimedes approximated pi and invented integral calculus precursors.
    Eratosthenes calculated the circumference of the Earth with remarkable
    accuracy using shadow angles and known distances.

NUMBERS_ROMAN: Roman numerals (I, V, X, L, C, D, M) were used throughout
    the Roman Empire (c. 500 BCE – 476 CE) for commerce, dates, and
    inscriptions.  They lack a zero and are additive/subtractive in logic:
    IV = 4, IX = 9, XL = 40, XC = 90, CD = 400, CM = 900.

NUMBERS_ARABIC_INDIAN: The Hindu-Arabic numeral system (0–9) originated in
    India (c. 500 CE) and was transmitted to Europe via Arab scholars such
    as Al-Khwarizmi (c. 820 CE), whose work "Al-Kitab al-mukhtasar fi
    hisab al-jabr wal-muqabala" gave us the word "algebra."
    Fibonacci introduced these numerals to Europe in "Liber Abaci" (1202).

NUMBERS_ZERO: Zero as a standalone number (not merely a placeholder) was
    formalised by the Indian mathematician Brahmagupta in 628 CE, who
    defined rules for arithmetic with zero.

NUMBERS_CALCULUS: Isaac Newton and Gottfried Leibniz independently invented
    calculus in the 17th century.  Newton called it "the method of
    fluxions"; Leibniz introduced the notation (∫, d/dx) still used today.

MATHEMATICS_THEORY_RELATIVITY: Albert Einstein's Special Theory of
    Relativity (1905) states that the laws of physics are the same for all
    non-accelerating observers and that the speed of light in a vacuum is
    constant (~299 792 458 m/s).  His famous equation E = mc² expresses
    the equivalence of mass and energy.  His General Theory (1915) extends
    this to gravity, describing it as curvature of spacetime caused by mass.

MATHEMATICS_NEWTON_GRAVITY: Isaac Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation
    (1687) states that every particle of matter attracts every other
    particle with a force proportional to the product of their masses and
    inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them:
    F = G × (m₁ × m₂) / r²
    This explained planetary orbits, tides, and projectile motion.

MATHEMATICS_PI: Pi (π ≈ 3.14159265...) is the ratio of a circle's
    circumference to its diameter.  It is irrational and transcendental —
    its decimal expansion never repeats or terminates.

[ASTRONOMY]
################################################################################

PLANET_MERCURY: Mercury — closest planet to the Sun. No atmosphere to speak
    of; surface temperatures swing from -180 °C to 430 °C.  No known moons.
    Composed largely of iron and rock.

PLANET_VENUS: Venus — hottest planet (surface ~465 °C) due to a thick CO₂
    atmosphere causing a runaway greenhouse effect.  Atmospheric pressure
    is 92× that of Earth.  No known moons.  Sulphuric acid clouds.

PLANET_EARTH: Earth — the only known planet harbouring life.  Atmosphere:
    78 % nitrogen, 21 % oxygen, 0.04 % CO₂, and trace gases.  One moon
    (Luna).  Age: ~4.5 billion years.

PLANET_MARS: Mars — the "Red Planet" due to iron oxide (rust) on its surface.
    Thin CO₂ atmosphere.  Two small moons: Phobos and Deimos.  Home to the
    tallest volcano in the solar system (Olympus Mons, ~21.9 km) and the
    longest canyon (Valles Marineris).

PLANET_JUPITER: Jupiter — largest planet; a gas giant composed mainly of
    hydrogen and helium.  Has at least 95 confirmed moons.  Major moons
    (Galilean): Io (volcanic), Europa (sub-surface ocean), Ganymede
    (largest moon in the solar system), Callisto (heavily cratered).
    Famous for its Great Red Spot — a storm larger than Earth ongoing for
    hundreds of years.

PLANET_SATURN: Saturn — famous for its ring system (mostly water ice and
    rock particles).  Gas giant, mostly hydrogen and helium.  83+ moons.
    Major moons: Titan (thick N₂/CH₄ atmosphere, liquid methane lakes),
    Enceladus (water geysers indicating a sub-surface ocean), Mimas, Rhea,
    Dione, Tethys, Iapetus.

PLANET_URANUS: Uranus — ice giant with a unique 98° axial tilt (rotates on
    its side).  Atmosphere: hydrogen, helium, methane (gives blue-green
    colour).  27 known moons named after Shakespeare and Alexander Pope
    characters (Miranda, Ariel, Umbriel, Titania, Oberon).  Faint rings.

PLANET_NEPTUNE: Neptune — farthest planet from the Sun; ice giant.
    Atmosphere: hydrogen, helium, methane.  Fastest winds in the solar
    system (~2 100 km/h).  16 known moons; largest is Triton (retrograde
    orbit, nitrogen geysers — likely a captured Kuiper Belt object).

DWARF_PLUTO: Pluto — reclassified as a dwarf planet in 2006 by the IAU.
    Located in the Kuiper Belt.  5 known moons: Charon, Styx, Nix,
    Kerberos, Hydra.  Thin N₂/CH₄/CO atmosphere.

ASTRONOMY_MOON_LANDING: On 20 July 1969, NASA's Apollo 11 mission landed
    astronauts Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin on the Moon.  Armstrong was
    the first human to step onto the lunar surface, stating:
    "That's one small step for [a] man, one giant leap for mankind."
    This event was reported by news organisations worldwide and confirmed
    by world leaders.  Melvin notes this fact and, once at Level 7,
    may evaluate supporting and conflicting evidence independently.

ASTRONOMY_HUBBLE: The Hubble Space Telescope (launched 1990) has produced
    images of objects over 13 billion light-years away, providing evidence
    that the universe is approximately 13.8 billion years old and expanding.

ASTRONOMY_JWST: The James Webb Space Telescope (launched 2021) observes
    in infrared and can detect the atmospheres of exoplanets, the first
    galaxies, and star-forming regions obscured by dust.

ASTRONOMY_BLACK_HOLES: A black hole is a region of spacetime where gravity
    is so strong that nothing — not even light — can escape past the event
    horizon.  They form from collapsed massive stars.  Supermassive black
    holes (millions to billions of solar masses) sit at the centres of most
    galaxies.  The first image of one was captured in 2019 (M87*).

[WORD_EXAMPLES]
# Step 5 — Dictionary words used correctly in sentences
################################################################################

WORD_THE: "The" is the definite article; it specifies a particular noun.
    Example: "The cat sat on the mat." (a specific cat, a specific mat)

WORD_A: "A" is the indefinite article; it refers to any one of a group.
    Example: "I saw a bird in a tree." (some bird, some tree)

WORD_ALGORITHM: An algorithm is a step-by-step procedure for solving a
    problem.  Example: "The sorting algorithm arranged the list in seconds."

WORD_BINARY: Binary describes a system with only two possible states: 0 and 1.
    Example: "Computers store all data in binary."

WORD_ENTROPY: Entropy is the measure of disorder or randomness in a system.
    Example: "As entropy increased, the system became more disordered."

WORD_HYPOTHESIS: A hypothesis is a testable proposed explanation for an
    observation.  Example: "Her hypothesis was that the plant would grow
    faster in sunlight."

WORD_ITERATION: Iteration is the repetition of a process.
    Example: "Each iteration of the loop brought us closer to the answer."

WORD_PARAMETER: A parameter is a variable that defines a characteristic
    of a system or function.
    Example: "The function accepts two parameters: name and age."

WORD_PERSISTENT: Persistent means continuing to exist over time despite
    obstacles.  Example: "Persistent memory retains data across sessions."

WORD_RECURSIVE: Recursive describes a process that refers to itself.
    Example: "A recursive function calls itself until a base case is met."

WORD_SYNTAX: Syntax refers to the set of rules that define valid code
    structure.  Example: "A missing semicolon caused a syntax error."

WORD_VARIABLE: A variable is a named storage location whose value can change.
    Example: "The variable 'count' was set to zero before the loop."

################################################################################

    Responses are very brief (one sentence or less).

HEAT_LEVEL_2: Very terse.  Key facts only.

HEAT_LEVEL_3: Concise.  Short paragraphs.

HEAT_LEVEL_4: Moderately concise with context.

HEAT_LEVEL_5: Balanced default.  Full explanations without padding.

HEAT_LEVEL_6: Detailed.  Includes examples where helpful.

HEAT_LEVEL_7: Comprehensive.  Explains background and nuances.

HEAT_LEVEL_8: Thorough.  Step-by-step breakdowns.

HEAT_LEVEL_9: Maximum verbosity.  Full encyclopaedic style with
    cross-references, history, and context.

THROTTLE_KEYBOARD: Press keys 1–9 at any time to adjust the heat level.
    Lower numbers = faster, more compact answers.
    Higher numbers = slower, more detailed answers.

THROTTLE_MENU: Press ESC, or type the sequence X, Y, Z, X, Y, Z once
    in order, to open the interactive control menu.

BINARY_ENCODING_NOTE: At heat level 1, key-value pairs from low-priority
    memories are encoded into binary (UTF-8 bytes → bit strings) before
    storage to minimise disk space usage.
